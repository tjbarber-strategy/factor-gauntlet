2. Executive Summary

The single biggest creative opportunity: Factor's most powerful consumer testimonials are emotional, not functional — people saved during depression, widows who stopped cooking, single parents stretched too thin. These generate the strongest organic sentiment in the review data but are completely absent from Factor's paid creative, which leans almost entirely on fitness/athlete/convenience positioning. An emotional use-case creative line would be net-new in the category.

The most dangerous blind spot: Every meal delivery brand leads with convenience and taste. These are table stakes, not differentiators. The real conversion barrier isn't "does this food taste good?" — it's "will I get trapped in a subscription I can't cancel?" Factor has 4,400+ BBB complaints predominantly about billing and cancellation friction. Any creative strategy that ignores this trust deficit is fighting with one hand behind its back.

The 2-3 consumer truths that should anchor first-round creative:

1. Factor's value argument only works when anchored against delivery app spending ($20-25/meal), not home cooking ($4/meal). Consumers who frame Factor as "cheaper than DoorDash" become long-term subscribers. Those who frame it as "more expensive than cooking" churn within a month. Creative must set the comparison frame before the viewer does it themselves.

2. The portions look small but are "deceptively filling." Multiple reviewers independently describe expecting to be hungry and being surprised they weren't. This is both the #2 visual objection (from non-buyers looking at photos) and a reframable advantage. An ad that deliberately triggers and then resolves this objection builds massive credibility.

3. Menu fatigue — not food quality — is the long-term churn trigger. Factor's food is genuinely good (positive reviews outnumber negative ~3:1). But "same flavors," "too much chicken," and "green beans with everything" are the slow-burn complaints that push 6-month subscribers to cancel. Creative emphasizing 100+ weekly options directly addresses the real retention killer.

Motivator hypothesis check: The Section 1 Problem Motivator-led hypothesis holds — consumer evidence overwhelmingly shows people buying Factor to escape a painful status quo (takeout guilt, no time to cook, unhealthy eating patterns). However, the Anti-Competitor Motivator signal is stronger than expected: the "villain" in most consumer stories isn't abstract time poverty — it's DoorDash/UberEats spending specifically. This makes Anti-Competitor creative (vs. delivery apps) a stronger first-round priority than originally hypothesized.
